The following is a list of notable deaths in June 2003.

Entries for each day are listed alphabetically by surname. A typical entry lists information in the following sequence:

  • Name, age, country of citizenship at birth, subsequent country of citizenship (if applicable), reason for notability, cause of death (if known), and reference.

June 2003[edit]

1[edit]

  • Sidney Bloom, 82, British restaurateur, founded the famous Bloom's kosher restaurant in London.[1]
  • Johnny Hopp, 86, American baseball player (St. Louis Cardinals, Boston Braves, Pittsburgh Pirates).[2]
  • Yevgeny Matveyev, 81, Russian actor and film director.
  • Pete Sivess, 89, American baseball player (Philadelphia Phillies).[3]
  • Eero Virtanen, 87, Finnish wrestler (men's lightweight wrestling at the 1948 Summer Olympics).[4]
  • Peter Yarranton, 78, English rugby player and administrator, Rugby Football Union president.[5]

2[edit]

  • Fred Blassie, 85, American professional wrestler, renowned as "The Hollywood Fashion Plate".[6]
  • Boycho Branzov, 57, Bulgarian basketball player (men's basketball at the 1968 Summer Olympics).[7]
  • Richard Cusack, 77, American actor (The Fugitive, High Fidelity, Chain Reaction).[8]
  • John Gerrard, 82, British police officer.
  • Donald Jack, 78, Canadian playwright and novelist.
  • Caryl Micklem, 79, British nonconformist minister and broadcaster.
  • John Robin Stephenson, 72, British army officer and cricket administrator, Secretary of MCC.
  • Ray Wehba, 86, American professional football player (USC, Brooklyn Dodgers, Green Bay Packers).[9]
  • James White, 89, Irish art expert and Director of the National Gallery of Ireland.[10]
  • J. R. Worsley, 79, British acupuncturist.[11]

3[edit]

  • Sir Anthony Barrowclough, 78, British lawyer and public servant, Parliamentary Commissioner for Administration.[12]
  • Peter Bromley, 74, British sports broadcaster, BBC Radio's voice of horse racing for 40 years (1961–2001).[13]
  • Felix de Weldon, 96, Austrian-American sculptor (Marine Corps War Memorial).[14]

4[edit]

  • Muhammad Abdul Bari, 72-73, Bangladeshi academic, linguist and Islamic scholar.[15]
  • Serafín Rojo, 77, Spanish cartoonist and painter.
  • Nurul Amin Talukdar, 57, Bangladeshi politician.
  • Shooby Taylor, 73, African American jazz vocalist.

5[edit]

  • Thomas Speakman Barnett, 93, Canadian politician (member of Parliament of Canada for Comox—Alberni, British Columbia).[16]
  • Sir John Fairclough, 72, British computer designer, Government Chief Scientific Adviser.
  • Jürgen Möllemann, 57, German minister.
  • Keijo Vanhala, 62, Finnish modern pentathlete (individual pentathlon, team pentathlon at the 1964 Summer Olympics).[17]
  • Meir Vilner, 84, Israeli politician, last surviving signatory to Israeli Declaration of Independence and chairman of the Communist Party of Israel.[18]

6[edit]

  • Ken Grimwood, 59, American author, heart attack.
  • Douglas Henson, 73, English cricketer.
  • Thomas Jackson, 78, British trade unionist.
  • Ray Medeiros, 77, American baseball player (Cincinnati Reds).[19]
  • Spurgeon Neel, 83, American U.S. Army physician, pioneered the development of battlefield casualty evacuation.[20]
  • Dave Rowberry, 62, English pianist and organist.
  • Shivnath Singh, 56, Indian long-distance runner (1976 Olympics men's marathon, 1980 Olympics men's marathon).[21]
  • Keith Winning, 75, Australian rugby player.

7[edit]

  • Stanley Betts, 91, English Anglican bishop (Dean of Rochester).[22]
  • R. W. G. Dennis, 92, British botanist.
  • Greg Garrett, 56, American baseball player (California Angels, Cincinnati Reds).[23]
  • Trevor Goddard, 40, British actor (JAG, Mortal Kombat, Pirates of the Caribbean: The Curse of the Black Pearl).
  • Tony McAuley, 63, BBC Northern Ireland broadcaster & filmmaker.
  • Roger Nelson, 47, American skydiver, founder of Skydive Chicago.[24]

8[edit]

  • Alun Davies, 80, British Anglican priest, Dean of Llandaff.
  • Herschel Burke Gilbert, 85, American composer.
  • Leighton Rees, 63, Welsh darts player.

9[edit]

  • Wilkie D. Ferguson, 65, American lawyer and judge (U.S. District Judge of the U.S. District Court for the Southern District of Florida), leukemia.[25]
  • David Grayden, 79, Australian politician.
  • Indradeep Sinha, 88, Indian freedom fighter and communist leader.
  • German Sveshnikov, 66, Soviet fencer (Olympic medals: 1960 gold medal, 1964 gold medal, 1968 silver medal).[26]
  • Sid Williams, 83, English footballer.

10[edit]

  • Charles Harrison Brown, 82, American politician (U.S. Representative for Missouri's 7th congressional district).[27]
  • Donald Regan, 84, Chief of Staff and Treasury Secretary during the Reagan administration.[28]
  • Sir Bernard Williams, 73, British moral philosopher.[29]
  • Phil Williams, 64, Welsh politician, member of the National Assembly for Wales for South Wales East.[30]

11[edit]

  • David Brinkley, 82, American broadcast journalist (The Huntley–Brinkley Report, NBC Nightly News, This Week with David Brinkley).[31]
  • Pankaj Charan Das, 78, Indian classical dancer.
  • Guy Willatt, 85, English cricketer.

12[edit]

  • Itamar Assumpção, 53, Brazilian songwriter and composer.[32]
  • Joseph L. Fleiss, 65, American professor of biostatistics.
  • Anna M. Louw, 89, South African author.
  • Gregory Peck, 87, American actor (To Kill a Mockingbird, Roman Holiday, The Yearling).[33]
  • Sam Schulman, 93, American sports businessman (Seattle SuperSonics, San Diego Chargers), blood disease.[34]

13[edit]

  • Harold Ashby, 78, American jazz tenor saxophonist (Duke Ellington Orchestra).[35]
  • Robin Russell, 14th Duke of Bedford, 63, British peer.[36]
  • Lucile Bluford, 91, American civil rights activist, editor and publisher (Kansas City Call).[37]
  • Lefty Hayden, 68, American baseball player (Cincinnati Redlegs).[38]
  • Silvio Pedroni, 85, Italian racing cyclist (men's individual road race, men's team road race at the 1948 Summer Olympics).[39]

14[edit]

  • James Cameron, 73, British professor of forensic medicine (FINA) and forensic scientist (A Cry in the Dark).[40]
  • Jimmy Knepper, 75, American jazz trombonist, complications of Parkinson's disease.
  • Anna Larsson, 81, Swedish athlete.
  • John Weld, 98, American newspaper reporter and writer (Don't You Cry for Me, Young Man in Paris, September Song).[41]
  • Dale Whittington, 43, American race car driver.[42]
  • Pete Wysocki, 54, American professional football player (Western Michigan, Washington Redskins).[43]

15[edit]

  • Enrico Baj, 78, Italian artist and art writer.[44]
  • Hume Cronyn, 91, Canadian-born American actor (The Seventh Cross, Cocoon, The Pelican Brief).[45]
  • Sir Ralph Kilner Brown, 93, British jurist and athlete.
  • Philip Stone, 79, British actor (The Shining, Indiana Jones and the Temple of Doom, A Clockwork Orange).[46]
  • Bill Wentworth, 95, Australian politician (member of Australian Parliament for Mackellar).[47]
  • Sir James Willis, 79, Australian admiral and Chief of Naval Staff.[48]

16[edit]

  • Asa Baber, 66, American writer and magazine columnist for Playboy.[49]
  • Les Benjamin, 78, Canadian politician (MP for Regina—Lake Centre, Regina West, Regina—Lumsden, Saskatchewan).[50]
  • John L. Grove, 82, American inventor and industrialist.
  • Marjorie Pyles Honzik, 95, American developmental psychologist.
  • Peter Redgrove, 71, British poet.
  • Carlos Rivas, 78, American actor.
  • Georg Henrik von Wright, 87, Finnish philosopher, professor and writer.[51]

17[edit]

  • Cheryl Byron, 56, Trinidad visual artist, dancer and singer.
  • Frank M. Clark, 87, American politician (U.S. Representative for Pennsylvania's 25th congressional district).[52]
  • Paul Hirst, 57, British sociologist and political theorist.
  • Robert M. Ricketts, 83, American orthodontist.
  • Grover C. Stephens, 78, American marine biologist and comparative physiologist.

18[edit]

  • Guy Bara, 79, Belgian comic strip writer and artist (Max l'explorateur).[53]
  • Sir Kenneth Cross, 91, British Royal Air Force commander.
  • Larry Doby, 79, American baseball player (Cleveland Indians) and member of the MLB Hall of Fame, second black man to play in the MLB.[54]

19[edit]

  • Jack Butterworth, Baron Butterworth, 85, British lawyer, academic and life peer (House of Lords 1985–2003).[55]
  • Glen Grant, 56, Hawaiian historian, folklorist and author.
  • Rafael Ileto, 82, Filipino army general and politician.
  • Laura Sadler, 22, British television actress.
  • Belding Hibbard Scribner, 82, American physician.

20[edit]

  • LaMar Baker, 87, American politician (U.S. Representative for Tennessee's 3rd congressional district).[56]
  • Fielder Cook, 80, American television and film director, producer, and writer, complications from a stroke.
  • Raymond Serra, 66, American actor.
  • Bob Stump, 76, American politician (U.S. Representative for Arizona's 3rd congressional district), myelodysplasia.[57]
  • Vic Vasicek, 77, American football player (Buffalo Bills, Los Angeles Rams).[58]
  • Peter Wright, 83, British potter and sculptor.[59]

21[edit]

  • George Axelrod, 81, American screenwriter (Bus Stop, Breakfast at Tiffany's, The Manchurian Candidate).[60]
  • Piet Dankert, 69, Dutch politician, State Secretary for Foreign Affairs and President of the European Parliament.
  • Charles Dédéyan, 93, French literary historian.
  • Jason Moran, 35, Australian criminal, murdered.
  • Roger Neilson, 69, Canadian ice hockey coach, head coach for eight different NHL teams from 1977 to 2002.[61]
  • Sergei Vronsky, 67, Soviet cinematographer [62]

22[edit]

  • Vasil Bykau, 79, Belarusian writer.
  • Joseph Chaikin, 67, American theatre director, actor, and playwright.
  • Brian Dillon, 77, British lawyer and judge.
  • Harry Kinzy, 92, American baseball player (Chicago White Sox).[63]
  • Shelby Starner, 19, American singer-songwriter and musician, complications from bulimia nervosa.

23[edit]

  • Maynard Jackson, 65, first African-American Mayor of Atlanta, Georgia.[64]
  • Doug Ring, 84, Australian cricketer.
  • Alexander Sidelnikov, 52, Soviet ice hockey player.
  • Bob Smith, 75, American baseball player (Boston Red Sox, Chicago Cubs, Cleveland Indians).[65]

24[edit]

  • Jack Bruner, 78, American baseball player (Chicago White Sox, St. Louis Browns).[66]
  • Wataru Kubo, 74, Japanese politician.
  • Leon Uris, 78, Jewish-American author.
  • Barbara Weeks, 89, American actress (Ziegfeld Follies, Now I'll Tell).[67]
  • Dee Wells, 78, American broadcaster, journalist and novelist.[68]

25[edit]

  • Rene Cayetano, 68, Filipino lawyer, television presenter, journalist and politician, abdominal cancer.
  • Johnny Dauwe, 37, Belgian Olympic cyclist.
  • Lester Maddox, 87, segregationist Governor of the State of Georgia, complications from pneumonia and prostate cancer.[69]
  • Warnasena Rasaputra, 75, Sri Lankan economist.
  • Preston Washington, 54, American minister in Harlem, known for opening his church to tourists.[70]
  • Shun Yashiro, 70, Japanese actor and voice actor, stroke.

26[edit]

  • John G. Adams, 91, American lawyer, counsel in the Army–McCarthy hearings.[71]
  • Marc-Vivien Foé, 28, Cameroonian footballer collapsed and died on the football pitch in Lyon.
  • Sir Denis Thatcher, 1st Baronet, 88, British businessman, Spouse of the Prime Minister (1979–1990).[72]
  • Strom Thurmond, 100, Governor of South Carolina, United States Republican Senator from South Carolina, Presidential candidate (as a Dixiecrat), and the only centenarian to serve in the U.S. Congress.[73]
  • Peter Waters, 73, British bookbinder and one of the world's leading authorities on book conservation.[74]
  • Philip Weekes, 83, Welsh mining engineer.[75]

27[edit]

  • Magne Aarøen, 59, Norwegian politician.
  • Gerald Balfour, 4th Earl of Balfour, 77, British hereditary peer (House of Lords 1968–1999), businessman and politician.[76]
  • Prince Carl Bernadotte, 92, Swedish prince.[77]
  • David Newman, 66, American screenwriter (Bonnie and Clyde, There Was a Crooked Man..., What's Up, Doc?).[78]
  • Ken Smith, 64, British poet.
  • Sir John Stokes, 85, British politician (Member of Parliament for Oldbury and Halesowen, Halesowen and Stourbridge).[79]

28[edit]

  • George Baxt, 80, American screenwriter and author (Circus of Horrors, The City of the Dead), complication from heart surgery.[80]
  • Kevin Belcher, 42, American professional football player (University of Texas at El Paso, New York Giants).[81]
  • Robert Muir Graves, 72, American golf course architect, cancer.
  • Rio Kishida, 57, Japanese playwright and director.
  • Joan Lowery Nixon, 76, American journalist and author, pancreatic cancer.[82]
  • Wim Slijkhuis, 80, Dutch athlete (two-time bronze medal winner at 1948 Summer Olympics: 1500 metres, 5000 metres).[83]

29[edit]

  • Rod Amateau, 79, American screenwriter and director (The Many Loves of Dobie Gillis, The George Burns and Gracie Allen Show), cerebral hemorrhage.[84]
  • Katharine Hepburn, 96, American actress (The African Queen, The Lion in Winter, On Golden Pond).[85]
  • Gregor MacGregor, 69, Scottish Anglican prelate, Bishop of Moray, Ross and Caithness.
  • Norman O'Connor, 81, American priest and jazz musician.

30[edit]

  • Noor Alam, 73, Pakistani field hockey player (Olympic field hockey: 1956 silver medal, 1960 gold medal).[86]
  • William J. J. Gordon, 83, American psychologist and inventor.
  • Buddy Hackett, 78, American comedian and actor (It's a Mad, Mad, Mad, Mad World, The Little Mermaid, The Love Bug).[87]
  • Robert McCloskey, 88, children's book writer and illustrator.
  • Constance Smith, 75, Irish actress.
