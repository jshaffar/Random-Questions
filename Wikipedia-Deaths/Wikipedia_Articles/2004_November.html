The following is a list of notable deaths in November 2004.

Entries for each day are listed alphabetically by surname. A typical entry lists information in the following sequence:

  • Name, age, country of citizenship at birth, subsequent country of citizenship (if applicable), reason for notability, cause of death (if known), and reference.

November 2004[edit]

1[edit]

  • James Hanson, Baron Hanson, 82, British industrialist and Conservative life peer, cancer.[1]
  • Hatem Kamil, Iraqi deputy governor of Baghdad, shot.[2]
  • Terry Knight, 61, American rock manager and producer (Grand Funk Railroad), shot during domestic dispute.[3]
  • Mark Ledford, 43/4, American trumpeter, singer and guitarist.[4]
  • Mac Dre, 34, American rapper, drive-by shooting.[5]

2[edit]

  • Sheikh Zayed bin Sultan Al Nahyan, 86, Emirati politician, president of UAE (1971–2004), Emir of Abu Dhabi.[6]
  • Gustaaf Joos, 81, Belgian Cardinal.[7]
  • Gerrie Knetemann, 53, Dutch cyclist (world champion, 1978), heart attack.[8]
  • Basil Thompson, 67, American ballet master.[9]
  • Theo van Gogh, 47, Dutch filmmaker, television presenter, and author, shot.[10]

3[edit]

  • James H. Binger, 88, American lawyer, entrepreneur and philanthropist.
  • Joe Bushkin, 87, American swing era jazz pianist, pneumonia.[11]
  • Sergejs Žoltoks, 31, Latvian ice hockey player, heart failure due to cardiac arrhythmia.[12]

4[edit]

  • Mohammed Bello, 74, Nigerian jurist, Chief Justice (1987–1995).[13]
  • Robert Heaton, 43, British songwriter and drummer (New Model Army), pancreatic cancer.[14]
  • Richard Hongisto, 67, American former sheriff of San Francisco and Cleveland, Ohio, heart attack.[15]
  • Ellen Meloy, 58, American author.[16]
  • Yasutomi Nishizuka, 72, Japanese biochemist, discovered Protein Kinase C (PKC).[17]
  • Dee Phillips, 85, American baseball player (Cincinnati Reds, Boston Braves).[18]
  • Delbert Plett, 56, Russian-Canadian lawyer and historian, known for the history of Russian Mennonites in Canada.[19]

5[edit]

  • Harold de Andrado, 76, Sri Lankan cricket writer.[20]
  • Donald Jones, 72, American-born Dutch actor, comedian, singer and dancer, first black Dutch celebrity, heart attack.[21]
  • Basil McIvor, 76, Northern Irish politician and educationalist.[22]

6[edit]

  • Fred Dibnah, 66, British steeplejack and television presenter, prostate cancer.[23]
  • Pete Jolly, 72, American jazz pianist and accordionist.[24]
  • Elizabeth Rogers, 70, American actress (Star Trek), multiple strokes and lung cancer.[25]
  • Marion Shilling, 93, American film actress, leading lady in 1930s B-Western films.[26]
  • Patrick F. Taylor, 67, American businessman, heart infection.[27]
  • Johnny Warren, 61, Australian soccer player, coach and ethnic community advocate, lung cancer.[28]
  • Déborah Weil, 47, Mexican Olympic diver[1]

7[edit]

  • Bobby Clatterbuck, 72, American football player.
  • Howard Keel, 85, American actor and singer (Kiss Me Kate, Annie Get Your Gun, Dallas), colon cancer.[29]
  • Gibson Kente, 72, South African playwright, AIDS.[30]
  • Herman Postma, 71, American scientist and educational leader (director of Oak Ridge National Laboratory).[31]

8[edit]

  • Eddie Charlton, 75, Australian snooker player.[32]
  • Lennox Miller, 58, Jamaican Olympic athlete, cancer.[33]
  • Melba Phillips, 97, American physicist and educator, coronary artery disease.[34]

9[edit]

  • Iris Chang, 36, American historian and author (The Rape of Nanking), suicide.[35]
  • Emlyn Hughes, 57, British footballer (Liverpool F.C., England), brain tumour.[36]
  • Stieg Larsson, 50, Swedish writer.[37]

10[edit]

  • Elizabeth Chater, 94, Canadian author of novels and poetry.
  • Katy de la Cruz, 97, Filipino singer.[38]
  • Şeref Görkey, 91, Turkish footballer and manager.
  • Walter Pukutiwara, Aboriginal artist.
  • Erna Rosenstein, 91, Polish surrealist painter and poet, arterial sclerosis.[39]

11[edit]

  • Dayton Allen, 85, American comedian, voice of Deputy Dawg and Mayor Phineas T. Bluster.[40]
  • Yasser Arafat, 75, Palestinian PLO leader, President of the Palestinian Authority, cause disputed, possible poisoning.[41]
  • Richard Dembo, 56, French César Award-winning director, intestinal obstruction.[42]
  • Henry Harvey Williams, 87, Vincentian lawyer and civil servant, Acting Governor-General.[43]

12[edit]

  • Lelio Marino, 69, Italian-born American entrepreneur, owner of Modern Continental group.[44]
  • Usko Meriläinen, 74, Finnish composer.[45]
  • Norman Rose, 88, American radio and TV actor (All My Children, voice of Juan Valdez).[46]
  • Stanisław Skalski, 89, Polish World War II fighter ace.[47]
  • Mike Smith, 62, British cricketer, heart attack.[48]

13[edit]

  • John Balance, 42, British musician (Coil), fall.[49]
  • Ellen Fairclough, 99, Canadian politician, first female cabinet minister.[50]
  • Russell "Ol' Dirty Bastard" Jones, 35, American rapper, drug overdose.[51]
  • Harry Lampert, 88, American comic book and advertising artist, co-creator of The Flash, author of instructional books on contract bridge, cerebral hemorrhage.[52]
  • Domenic Mobilio, 35, Canadian soccer player, heart attack.[53]
  • Carlo Rustichelli, 87, Italian film composer.[54]
  • Roy Thomas, 54, Canadian aboriginal artist, cancer.[55]
  • Keith Weller, 58, English footballer (Millwall. Leicester City), cancer.[56]

14[edit]

  • Michel Colombier, 65, French composer, cancer.[57]
  • David Stanley Evans, 86, Welsh astronomer.[58]
  • Jesse Gonder, 68, American baseball player (New York Yankees, Cincinnati Reds, New York Mets, Milwaukee Braves, Pittsburgh Pirates).[59]
  • Evelyn West, 80, American burlesque stripper, pin-up girl and actress.[60]

15[edit]

  • Elmer L. Andersen, 96, American businessman, governor of Minnesota (1961–1963).[61]
  • Sir Bob Cooper, 68, Northern Irish politician.[62]
  • Colin Coulthard, 83, British Royal Air Force officer.[63]
  • John Morgan, 74, Welsh-born Canadian comedian, former member of the Royal Canadian Air Farce, heart attack.[64]
  • Jack Schmidt, 80, Canadian professional ice hockey player (Boston Bruins).[65]

16[edit]

  • Yves Berger, 73, French writer and editor.[66]
  • Massimo Freccia, 98, Italian-American conductor.[67]
  • Margaret Hassan, 59, British aid worker, chief of the humanitarian relief organization CARE International, presumed killed by hostage takers in Iraq.[68]
  • Reed Irvine, 82, American economist, founder of Accuracy in Media, complications of stroke.[69]

17[edit]

  • Floyd Baker, 88, American baseball player (St. Louis Browns, Chicago White Sox, Washington Senators, Boston Red Sox, Philadelphia Phillies).[70]
  • Mikael Ljungberg, 34, Swedish wrestler and Olympic gold medalist, suicide by hanging.[71]
  • Alexander Ragulin, 63, Soviet ice hockey player, 10-time IIHF World Champion and three-time Olympic gold medalist.[72]
  • Lena Townsend, 93, British politician, leader of the Inner London Education Authority (1969–1970).

18[edit]

  • Danilo Anderson, 38, Venezuelan prosecutor, bombing.[73]
  • Juan Carlos Aramburu, 92, Argentinian Roman Catholic Archbishop of Buenos Aires (1975–1990), Cardinal since 1976.[74]
  • Robert Bacher, 99, American nuclear physicist, co-leader of the Manhattan Project.[75]
  • Frank Baldwin, 75, American baseball player (Cincinnati Redlegs).[76]
  • Bobby Frank Cherry, 75, American criminal, convicted in the 16th Street Baptist Church bombing, cancer.[77]
  • Cy Coleman, 76, American composer of Broadway musicals, heart attack.[78]
  • Alfred Maseng, Vanuatuan president (1994, 2004) and foreign minister (1995–1996).[citation needed]
  • N. Mathrubootham, 60, Indian psychiatrist and actor.[79]
  • Antonio Pocovi, 82, Argentine Olympic sprinter (men's 400 metres and men's 4 × 400 metres relay at the 1948 Summer Olympics).[80]
  • George Scholes, 75, Canadian Olympic hockey player (bronze medal winner in men's ice hockey at the 1956 Winter Olympics).[81]

  • Don MacMillan, 76, Australian Olympic athlete.[85]
  • Terry Melcher, 62, American musician and producer, son of Doris Day, melanoma.[86]
  • Brian Traxler, 37, American baseball player.[87]
